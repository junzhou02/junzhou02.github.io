Page story

From pure mathematics to systems that move in the real world

The starting point

My undergraduate training in mathematics gave me a habit of looking for internal structure: geometry, approximation, analysis, and the logic underneath formal systems. That habit still shapes how I do research today.

The turn

Over time, I became more interested in systems that evolve, interact, and resist closed-form answers. That led me from abstract mathematics toward control, signal modeling, stochastic dynamics, and operator-theoretic viewpoints.

The present

My recent work studies how to identify, approximate, and regulate difficult systems: infinite-dimensional linear systems, stochastic dynamical systems, and noisy nonlinear processes whose behavior becomes clearer in the right coordinates.

The way I like to work

I enjoy crossing disciplines and environments. Each place I studied in left a different mark on my thinking: mathematical depth, engineering pragmatism, operator theory, and a stronger instinct for asking questions that matter beyond a single model.

Research board

Questions I keep returning to

01

Infinite-dimensional control

How can feedback optimization operate in real time when the dynamics come from PDEs and the control law must respect both rigor and computation?

02

Stochastic system identification

When observations are noisy, can we still recover the generator, invariant structure, or interpretable evolution operator behind a stochastic process?

03

Koopman-inspired representation

I am drawn to spectral and operator-theoretic descriptions because they can turn nonlinear motion into something more analyzable, communicable, and useful.

Current notes

Work in progress

Feedback optimization for infinite-dimensional linear systems

I am studying a framework for real-time optimization and regulation in PDE-driven systems, with an emphasis on input-to-state stability, Lyapunov analysis, and explicit performance guarantees.

Resolvent-Yosida identification of stochastic systems

Another recent direction asks whether the Koopman generator can be learned from noisy trajectories in a way that preserves both predictive power and operator-level meaning.
